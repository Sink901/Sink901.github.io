Professional Reflection

By Zdena Sinkhorn

Ever since I can remember, I have had an affinity for processing and organizing information. It started when I was young with categorizing my books by color and ranking, and then evolved into categorizing my notes and documents in my later years. It was only during my freshmen year at the College of Wooster that this odd interest inspired my future career path. Working with the College’s Special Collections Department truly changed how I approach and understand organization when it relates to information. Prior to this, I had considered the way I process and organize things restricted to physical materials. But after experiencing work in an archive, I realized the principles of sorting could be applied to my professional life and to theoretical information.

I took this realization and applied it to one of my favorite summer internships, my time with the North Carolina Maritime Museum. It was there that I learned more about why it is so critical to have an understanding of why it is important to sort and organize information. Without somebody there to process and store all of the artifacts, they would not contribute anything to the museum. I took this knowledge and applied it back to my work with the College, helping the collections to retain the consistency that is critical for an archive to function. By seeing what the impact of clear information organization is on real-world scenarios, I was able to develop a more nuanced approach to both information and how individuals may approach that information. My time spent working with the museum’s records showed me that not everyone approaches an object in the same way and will not consider describing it in the same manner. By developing a consistent standard for processing information, it becomes easier to retain the necessary information and dispense with the information that is not needed.

Identifying how my passion for information processing could be applied to real-world scenarios was a significant part of my career development. After working for several museums and archives, I became aware of how this type of specialty could be used in a range of jobs. I tried collections management, archival management, librarian, data processing, and any other job that would allow me to increase my knowledge about the information sciences. While I have enjoyed my work across all these positions, I still favor the archival tract as my main job focus. There is something inspirational about being a part of an archive. From how the collections are organized and accessible to how specifically useful they can be, working in any archive has always brought me joy and fulfillment. Even more significantly, I have found myself to be more than capable of handling the different job expectations that come with working in an archive.

While having a permanent position in an archive, museum, or data facility is still a way off for me, I have found fulfillment in contract and internship work. My range of different positions has given me a breadth of insight into the job field. I plan to finish my master’s degree and then pursue a temporary position with the National Archives and Records Administration. I hope to attain a full-time position as the archivist of a bank or local library branch, as I find those specialties to be particularly fulfilling. It is satisfying to know that what began as an odd childhood interest has now bloomed into a full career path. Not only that, but it is a path that I have already made a good amount of progress on. I plan to continue to pursue my passion for archiving and books through the competition of my graduate degree before pursuing a full-time position with an institution. I am happy with the work I have done so far and I look forward to what adventures await me in the future.
